# Skill — Conclusion & Limitation

These closing sections serve distinct purposes: the Conclusion distills the contribution into qualitative language, Limitations demonstrate intellectual honesty, and Future Work shows the authors understand what comes next. Each has its own constraints.

## When to invoke

- Drafting or polishing Conclusion / Limitation / Future Work sections.

## Conclusion

A single paragraph — like the Abstract, no multi-paragraph Conclusions. Rephrase the contribution in qualitative language; do not copy-paste from the Introduction. No citations and no specific numbers (same constraint as abstract-writing).

## Limitations

Mandatory — reviewers value honest self-assessment. Include at least 2–3 specific limitations. Do not disguise known deficiencies as "future work"; a limitation is something the current work cannot do, stated plainly.

## Future Work

Provide 1–2 concrete directions with enough specificity that a reader could act on them. Vacuous phrases such as "opens exciting new avenues" or "promising directions for future research" carry no information and signal that the authors have not thought beyond the current paper.

## Venue-specific additions

If required by the venue (NeurIPS/ICML/ICLR), include an ethics statement and a reproducibility statement as separate subsections.

## Pitfalls

- Conclusion spanning 3 paragraphs.
- Limitations too vague ("may not generalize to all domains").
- Future Work consisting of empty platitudes ("opens exciting new avenues").
