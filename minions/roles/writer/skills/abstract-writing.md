# Skill — Abstract Writing

Write a layered scientific abstract from broad context to result, knowledge delta, and significance.

## Core move

Draft the abstract as a constrained narrative ladder: field introduction, related-discipline background, study problem, main result, knowledge delta, and broader context. Each claim must be traceable to the manuscript or to Expert-provided interpretation.

## Procedure

1. **Gather claim sources.** Read the current introduction, method summary, results, and Expert notes before drafting. Do not invent motivations, novelty, or implications that are not supported.
2. **Open with broad context.** Write one or two sentences giving a basic introduction to the field, comprehensible to a scientist in any discipline.
3. **Narrow to technical background.** Write two or three sentences with more detailed background, still comprehensible to scientists in related disciplines.
4. **State the study problem.** Write one sentence clearly naming the general problem addressed by this particular study.
5. **State the main result.** Write one sentence summarizing the main result, using "here we show" or an equivalent direct phrase.
6. **Explain the knowledge delta.** Write two or three sentences explaining what the main result reveals in direct comparison to what was thought previously, or how it adds to previous knowledge.
7. **Close with general context.** Write one or two sentences placing the result into a broader scientific or practical context.
8. **Audit evidence and length.** Remove unsupported claims, vague impact language, and method detail that crowds out the result. If the venue has a word limit, tighten to that limit after the structure is complete.

## When to invoke

- Drafting or rewriting the paper abstract.
- Aligning the abstract with a new main result, revised claim scope, or reviewer-requested framing change.
- Preparing a general-audience abstract for a broad scientific venue.

## Pitfalls

- Starting with method mechanics instead of the field-level motivation.
- Claiming impact, novelty, or comparison to prior work without evidence from the manuscript or Expert.
- Burying the main result in background prose, or omitting an explicit "here we show" sentence.
- Writing only for specialists when the target venue expects broad scientific readability.

## Output habit

Keep the abstract in `workspace/paper/` with sentence-level support noted in nearby draft comments or notes when claims are non-obvious, using `[derived: section <N>]`, `[derived: Expert note <id>]`, or `[derived: artifact <path>]` per root §9.
